## Paper 1: LLMs Cannot Find Reasoning Errors, but Can Correct Them Given the Error Location

- **Title:** LLMs cannot find reasoning errors, but can correct them given the error location
- **Authors:** Gladys Tyen, Hassan Mansoor, Victor Carbune, Peter Chen, Tony Mak
- **Affiliations:** University of Cambridge; Google Research
- **Year:** 2024 (arXiv: 2311.08516v3, June 2024)

### Research Question / Hypothesis
The paper investigates why LLM self-correction fails on reasoning tasks. The central hypothesis is that poor self-correction performance stems from LLMs' inability to *find* logical mistakes, rather than an inability to *correct* a known mistake. The authors decompose self-correction into two components: mistake finding and output correction.

### Methodology
1. **Mistake-finding benchmark:** Created BIG-Bench Mistake, a dataset of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) traces generated by PaLM 2 Unicorn, annotated with the location of the first logical error.
2. **Three prompting methods for mistake finding:**
   - Direct trace-level prompting (single token output for whole trace)
   - Direct step-level prompting (binary Yes/No per step)
   - CoT step-level prompting (full reasoning chain per step -- most resource-intensive)
3. **Backtracking method for correction:** Feed oracle (ground truth) mistake location to the model, regenerate from that step at temperature=1, then complete remaining steps at temperature=0.
4. **Trained classifier:** Fine-tuned PaLM 2 Otter on out-of-domain data to find mistakes, demonstrating transferability.

### Datasets Used
- **BIG-Bench Mistake** (new dataset, released):
  - 2,186 CoT-style traces across 5 tasks from BIG-Bench: word sorting, tracking shuffled objects, logical deduction, multi-step arithmetic, Dyck languages
  - Generated with PaLM 2 Unicorn at temperature=0
  - Annotated with location of first logical error
  - Sampled to contain ~15% correct and ~85% incorrect traces
  - Available at: https://github.com/WHGTyen/BIG-Bench-Mistake

### Key Findings and Results

**Mistake-finding accuracy (overall):**
| Model | Direct (trace) | Direct (step) | CoT (step) |
|-------|---------------|---------------|------------|
| GPT-4 | 39.80% | **52.87%** | 43.40% |
| GPT-4-Turbo | 30.13% | 48.33% | -- |
| GPT-3.5-Turbo | 10.44% | 14.78% | 14.31% |
| PaLM 2 Unicorn | 17.09% | 23.67% | 24.65% |
| Gemini Pro | 16.14% | -- | -- |

- **Best performance:** GPT-4 with direct step-level prompting at only 52.87% overall accuracy.
- **Human annotators** achieve high agreement (Cohen's kappa ~0.90 for 4/5 tasks), demonstrating the task is unambiguous.
- **Trade-off observed:** More complex prompting methods (CoT step-level) increase sensitivity to finding mistakes but dramatically reduce accuracy on traces with no mistakes (high false positive rate).

**Correction via backtracking (with oracle mistake location):**
| Task | Delta accuracy (correct traces) | Delta accuracy (incorrect traces) |
|------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Word sorting | -11.11% | +23.53% |
| Tracking shuffled objects | -6.67% | +43.92% |
| Logical deduction | -11.43% | +36.86% |
| Multistep arithmetic | -0.00% | +18.04% |
| Dyck languages | -6.82% | +18.06% |

- Gains from correcting incorrect traces consistently exceed losses from changing correct answers.
- Random baseline (random mistake location) yields much smaller gains, confirming the value of accurate mistake location.

**Trained classifier:** A small PaLM 2 Otter classifier trained on out-of-domain data outperforms prompting large models for mistake finding.

### Code/Data Availability
- Dataset and code: https://github.com/WHGTyen/BIG-Bench-Mistake (Apache License 2.0)

### Relevance to LLM Self-Evaluation of Reasoning and Systematic Blindspots
**Highly relevant.** This paper directly demonstrates a core systematic blindspot: LLMs cannot reliably identify their own reasoning errors, even in objective, unambiguous cases. The finding that the bottleneck in self-correction is mistake *finding* (not mistake *correction*) is a fundamental insight for understanding LLM self-evaluation limitations. The paper shows that even GPT-4 achieves only ~53% accuracy at finding reasoning errors -- barely above chance for some tasks. This directly supports the thesis that LLMs have systematic blindspots in self-detection of logical fallacies.

## Paper 2: A Closer Look at the Self-Verification Abilities of Large Language Models in Logical Reasoning

- **Title:** A Closer Look at the Self-Verification Abilities of Large Language Models in Logical Reasoning
- **Authors:** Ruixin Hong, Hongming Zhang, Xinyu Pang, Dong Yu, Changshui Zhang
- **Affiliations:** Tsinghua University (THUAI); Tencent AI Lab
- **Year:** 2024 (EMNLP 2024)

### Research Question / Hypothesis
Can LLMs accurately identify logical fallacies in reasoning steps? The paper examines whether LLMs can reliably self-verify by testing their ability to identify fallacious reasoning, classify fallacy types, and whether providing fallacy definitions improves performance.

### Methodology
1. **Dataset construction (FALLACIES):** Created a hierarchical taxonomy of 232 types of logical fallacies. For each type, generated 10 fallacious and 10 correct reasoning steps. Reasoning steps have explicit premises and conclusions.
2. **Three experimental tasks:**
   - Binary identification: Is the reasoning step correct or fallacious?
   - Fallacy type classification: Classify into one of 232 types
   - Definition-aided identification: Does providing fallacy definitions help?
3. **Zero-shot evaluation** with a carefully designed prompt ("Is the following reasoning step correct? You can only answer Yes or No.").
4. **Tested 19 models** including GPT-4, GPT-3.5, Llama2 (7B/13B/70B), Vicuna, WizardLM, Flan-T5, Falcon, Baichuan2, ChatGLM, InternLM, Qwen-14B.

### Datasets Used
- **FALLACIES** (new dataset):
  - 232 types of reasoning fallacies in a hierarchical taxonomy
  - 4,640 reasoning steps total (2,320 fallacious + 2,320 correct)
  - Fallacy categories: Formal (proposition, quantification, syllogism, probability) and Informal (ambiguity, inconsistency, irrelevance, insufficiency, inappropriateness)
  - Quality check: 3 additional experts annotated 50 random samples with Cohen's Kappa = 0.856

### Key Findings and Results

**Binary identification accuracy (is the step correct/fallacious?):**
| Model | Formal Avg. | Informal Avg. | Overall Avg. |
|-------|------------|--------------|-------------|
| GPT-4 | 88.2% | 87.2% | **87.7%** |
| GPT-3.5 | 74.1% | 87.9% | 81.0% |
| Qwen-14B | 74.7% | 88.2% | 81.5% |
| Llama2-70B | 73.3% | 88.2% | 80.7% |
| Vicuna-13B | 69.8% | 86.4% | 78.1% |

- Most LLMs achieve 60-80% accuracy on this binary task.
- **Formal fallacies are harder** than informal fallacies for most models (except GPT-4 which excels on formal).
- GPT-4's formal fallacy accuracy (88.2%) is 13.5% higher than the next-best model.
- **Performance is remarkably imbalanced across fallacy types:** e.g., Qwen-14B achieves 91.7% on inconsistency but only 67.5% on probability fallacies.

**Fallacy type classification (232 types):**
| Model | Formal | Informal | Overall |
|-------|--------|----------|---------|
| GPT-4 | 41.2% | 28.7% | **35.0%** |
| GPT-3.5 | 21.6% | 12.9% | 17.3% |
| All other models | <10% | <10% | <10% |

- This task is extremely challenging. Most models score below 10%.
- Interesting reversal: models do *better* on formal than informal fallacy classification, opposite to identification performance. Authors hypothesize models may "know the names" of fallacies but not deeply understand them.

**Providing fallacy definitions hurts most models:**
- For most models, performance *decreases* when definitions are provided. E.g., Vicuna-13B drops from 78.1% to 64.6% (-13.5%), Llama2-70B drops from 80.7% to 55.4% (-25.3%).
- Only GPT-4 maintains performance (87.7% to 88.0%, +0.3%).
- Authors suggest definitions may not co-occur with fallacies in training data, and the added prompt complexity interferes with decision-making.

### Code/Data Availability
- Dataset available (referenced in paper, footnote 1)

### Relevance to LLM Self-Evaluation of Reasoning and Systematic Blindspots
**Directly relevant.** This paper provides the most comprehensive evaluation of LLMs' ability to identify specific types of logical fallacies. Key findings for our research:
1. Even GPT-4 at 87.7% on single-step identification would compound to poor performance on multi-step arguments (exponential degradation).
2. Massive performance imbalances across fallacy types reveal systematic blindspots -- models are good at detecting some fallacies and terrible at others.
3. The paradox that providing definitions hurts performance suggests LLMs do not deeply understand the fallacies they can sometimes identify.
4. The 232-type hierarchical taxonomy is directly useful for categorizing blindspots.

## Paper 3: Self-Contradictory Reasoning Evaluation and Detection

- **Title:** Self-Contradictory Reasoning Evaluation and Detection
- **Authors:** Ziyi Liu, Soumya Sanyal, Isabelle Lee, Yongkang Du, Rahul Gupta, Yang Liu, Jieyu Zhao
- **Affiliations:** University of Southern California; Amazon AGI
- **Year:** 2024 (appears in proceedings, arXiv 2023)

### Research Question / Hypothesis
Two fundamental questions: (1) How consistent is LLM reasoning (how often does the reasoning contradict the answer)? (2) Can models detect unreliable/self-contradictory reasoning? The paper investigates "SELF-CONTRA" reasoning where the model's reasoning does not support its final answer.

### Methodology
1. **Define SELF-CONTRA reasoning:** Cases where generated reasoning does not logically support the generated answer.
2. **Categorize into types:**
   - Type 1: Correct answer + correct (but imperfect) reasoning (evidence missing, incomplete reasoning)
   - Type 2: Correct answer + wrong reasoning (questionable cause, begging the question, wrong context knowledge)
   - Type 3: Wrong answer + wrong reasoning (standard errors)
   - RR: Right answer + right reasoning (ideal case)
   - WW: Wrong answer + wrong reasoning
3. **Evaluation across 3 datasets, 3 LLMs, 4 prompt settings** (zero-shot answer-first, zero-shot reason-first, few-shot answer-first, few-shot reason-first).
4. **Automatic detection:** Three methods tested using GPT-4:
   - I+R->O: Feed reasoning back and check if answer changes
   - Binary detection: Direct prompting for SELF-CONTRA prediction
   - FGE (Finer-Grained Enhanced): Classify specific fallacy categories then ensemble

### Datasets Used
- **WinoBias** (coreference resolution with gender bias): 353 data points in test set
- **WinoGender** (similar to WinoBias): gender-related coreference
- **WinoGrande** (commonsense coreference): 357 data points
- Total: 1,800 initial data points across all settings; 2,126 data points for finer-grained analysis with human annotation

### Key Findings and Results

**Self-Contradictory Rate (SCR) by model and dataset (answer-first, few-shot):**
| Model | WinoBias | WinoGender | WinoGrande |
|-------|----------|------------|------------|
| GPT-3.5 | 0.32 | 0.26 | 0.18 |
| Mistral 7B | 0.50 | 0.26 | 0.26 |
| Claude 3 | 0.10 | 0.02 | 0.04 |

- SELF-CONTRA is common: up to 50% of Mistral 7B outputs on WinoBias.
- Claude 3 has significantly lower SCR (0.02-0.10) compared to other models.
- WinoBias and WinoGender (social bias tasks) show more SELF-CONTRA than WinoGrande.
- Zero-shot prompting produces higher SCR than few-shot.

**Finer-grained analysis of SELF-CONTRA cases:**
- **Begging the question** is the most common wrong reasoning category (62-80% of Type 2 cases for GPT-3.5).
- Evidence missing and incomplete reasoning dominate Type 1 (correct but imperfect reasoning).
- Few-shot prompting mitigates evidence missing and wrong context knowledge but NOT begging the question.

**GPT-4's ability to detect SELF-CONTRA (F1 scores):**
| Detection Method | WB Anti | WB Pro | WinoGrande |
|-----------------|---------|--------|------------|
| I+R->O (GPT-3.5 outputs) | 0.208 | 0.065 | 0.289 |
| Binary (GPT-3.5 outputs) | 0.253 | 0.233 | 0.360 |
| FGE (GPT-3.5 outputs) | **0.522** | **0.628** | **0.504** |

- **GPT-4 achieves only ~52.2% F1** (best method: FGE) for detecting SELF-CONTRA, compared to **66.7% F1 for human annotators**.
- GPT-4 detects "begging the question" more effectively than other categories.
- GPT-4 consistently fails to detect "wrong context knowledge" (F1 = 0.000 across all datasets).

### Code/Data Availability
- Not explicitly mentioned; annotations appear to be manual.

### Relevance to LLM Self-Evaluation of Reasoning and Systematic Blindspots
**Highly relevant.** This paper demonstrates that:
1. LLMs frequently generate self-contradictory reasoning (up to 50% of the time for some model/task combinations), where the reasoning does not support the answer.
2. Even GPT-4 struggles to detect these contradictions (52.2% F1 vs. 66.7% human F1), revealing a fundamental self-evaluation blindspot.
3. The finding that "begging the question" is the dominant fallacy and is resistant to few-shot mitigation is a specific systematic blindspot.
4. The finer-grained categorization (QC, BQ, WCK) provides a vocabulary for describing types of reasoning failures that LLMs exhibit.
5. The disparity between accuracy (models often get the right answer) and reasoning quality (reasoning is often self-contradictory) is central to the "blindspot" concept.

## Paper 4: Faithful Chain-of-Thought Reasoning

- **Title:** Faithful Chain-of-Thought Reasoning
- **Authors:** Qing Lyu, Shreya Havaldar, Adam Stein, Li Zhang, Delip Rao, Eric Wong, Marianna Apidianaki, Chris Callison-Burch
- **Affiliations:** University of Pennsylvania
- **Year:** 2023 (published at ACL 2023)

### Research Question / Hypothesis
Standard CoT reasoning chains do not necessarily reflect how the model arrives at the answer (faithfulness problem). The authors propose "Faithful CoT," a two-stage framework that guarantees the reasoning chain provides a faithful explanation of the final answer by translating natural language into symbolic reasoning chains solved by deterministic solvers.

### Methodology
1. **Two-stage pipeline:**
   - **Translation stage:** LM translates natural language query into a reasoning chain interleaving natural language (CNL) with symbolic language (CSL -- Python, Datalog, PDDL)
   - **Problem Solving stage:** Deterministic solver (Python interpreter, Datalog engine, PDDL planner, relational inference engine) executes the symbolic reasoning chain
2. **CNL components:** Subquestions, dependency graphs, rationales
3. **Evaluated across 4 task domains:** Math Word Problems, Multi-hop QA, Planning, Relational Inference
4. **Compared to:** Standard prompting, CoT (Wei et al., 2022), Least-to-Most (Zhou et al., 2022)
5. **Two decoding strategies:** Greedy decoding and self-consistency decoding

### Datasets Used (10 benchmarks across 4 domains)
- **Math Word Problems:** GSM8K, SVAMP, MultiArith, ASDiv, AQuA
- **Multi-hop QA:** StrategyQA, Date Understanding (BIG-bench), Sports Understanding (BIG-bench)
- **Planning:** SayCan
- **Relational Inference:** CLUTRR

### Key Findings and Results

**Accuracy with Greedy Decoding (code-davinci-002):**
| Method | GSM8K | SVAMP | MultiArith | ASDiv | AQuA | SayCan | StrategyQA | Date | Sport | CLUTRR |
|--------|-------|-------|-----------|-------|------|--------|-----------|------|-------|--------|
| Standard | 19.6 | 69.5 | 43.8 | 72.1 | 31.5 | 82.5 | 63.9 | 51.3 | 71.9 | 42.0 |
| CoT | 63.3 | 77.3 | 96.5 | 80.0 | 42.1 | 86.4 | 72.5 | 59.9 | 98.6 | 48.5 |
| LtM | 38.3 | 80.3 | 74.0 | 76.5 | 40.6 | 77.7 | 72.2 | 76.6 | 99.5 | 47.2 |
| **Faithful CoT** | **72.3** | **83.4** | **98.8** | **80.2** | **47.2** | **89.3** | 63.0 | **81.6** | 99.1 | **58.9** |

- Faithful CoT outperforms all baselines on **8 of 10 datasets** with greedy decoding.
- Relative improvements: **+6.3%** on MWP, **+3.4%** on Planning, **+5.5%** on Multi-hop QA, **+21.4%** on Relational Inference.
- With self-consistency decoding: outperforms on **7 of 10 datasets**.
- With GPT-4: sets **new SOTA on 7 datasets** (95.0+ accuracy on 6 of them).
- Larger gains on harder datasets (e.g., +14% on GSM8K, the hardest MWP dataset).
- StrategyQA is the main weakness: Datalog syntax errors account for 29% of failures.

### Code/Data Availability
- Code and prompts available in their repository (referenced but URL not in extracted text).

### Relevance to LLM Self-Evaluation of Reasoning and Systematic Blindspots
**Moderately relevant.** This paper addresses the faithfulness problem from the solution side: standard CoT chains are not faithful to the model's actual reasoning process, so the authors propose ensuring faithfulness by construction through symbolic reasoning. Key relevance:
1. Demonstrates that standard CoT is fundamentally unfaithful -- the reasoning chain may not reflect how the model actually derives its answer.
2. The existence and success of this approach implies that standard self-evaluation of CoT reasoning is unreliable.
3. Provides evidence that when reasoning is made faithful (via deterministic solvers), performance improves, suggesting standard CoT's unfaithfulness is actively harmful.
4. The finding that gains are largest on harder tasks suggests faithfulness problems compound with reasoning complexity.

## Paper 5: Measuring Chain of Thought Faithfulness by Unlearning Reasoning Steps (FUR)

- **Title:** Measuring Chain of Thought Faithfulness by Unlearning Reasoning Steps
- **Authors:** Martin Tutek, Fateme Hashemi Chaleshtori, Ana Marasovic, Yonatan Belinkov
- **Affiliations:** Technion - Israel Institute of Technology; University of Utah
- **Year:** 2025 (arXiv)

### Research Question / Hypothesis
Is the reasoning verbalized in a Chain of Thought (CoT) faithful to the model's parametric beliefs (internal reasoning)? The paper introduces a framework for measuring "parametric faithfulness" -- whether the CoT actually reflects the model's internal reasoning process -- using targeted knowledge unlearning.

### Methodology
1. **Parametric Faithfulness Framework (PFF):** Two stages -- (1) parameter intervention (modify model weights) and (2) evaluation (measure effect on predictions).
2. **FUR (Faithfulness by Unlearning Reasoning steps):**
   - Erase information from individual CoT reasoning steps using NPO+KL (preference-optimization unlearning)
   - Only update FF2 matrices of Transformer MLPs
   - Unlearn each step individually for 5 iterations
   - Measure whether prediction changes when a reasoning step is unlearned
3. **Two faithfulness metrics:**
   - **FF-HARD** (binary): Does unlearning any step change the model's prediction? (lower bound on faithfulness)
   - **FF-SOFT** (continuous): How much probability mass shifts from the initial answer when a step is unlearned?
4. **Controls:** Efficacy (probability reduction of unlearned step), Specificity (unchanged predictions on held-out data), General capabilities (MMLU performance post-unlearning)
5. **Compared to Add-mistake baseline** (Lanham et al., 2023): Introduce mistakes into CoT via another LLM and check if prediction changes.

### Datasets Used
- **5 multi-choice QA datasets (250 instances each from test splits):**
  - OpenbookQA (science knowledge)
  - ARC-Challenge (science questions)
  - StrategyQA (multi-hop open-domain)
  - TruthfulQA (truthfulness)
  - Sports Understanding (BigBench-Hard, 248 instances)

### Models Tested
- LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct, LLaMA-3.2-3B-Instruct, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, Phi-3-mini-4k-Instruct

### Key Findings and Results

**Unlearning controls (averaged across datasets):**
| Model | Efficacy (Eff) | Specificity (Spec) | MMLU (Gen) |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|------------|
| LLaMA-8B | 20.8-48.3% | 95.7-98.3% | ~63.8% (base: 63.9%) |
| LLaMA-3B | 28.9-36.6% | 95.9-98.1% | ~60.2-60.4% |
| Mistral-2 | 48.6-72.1% | 94.8-97.6% | ~58.8-59.0% |
| Phi-3 | 11.0-44.2% | 97.0-99.5% | ~69.6-69.9% |

- Unlearning is precise: high specificity (>94%) and general capabilities preserved.

**FF-HARD: % of instances where unlearning changes prediction (FUR vs Add-mistake):**
| Model | ARC-ch FUR | ARC-ch +mistake | OpenbookQA FUR | OpenbookQA +mistake | StrategyQA FUR | StrategyQA +mistake | TruthfulQA FUR | TruthfulQA +mistake |
|-------|-----------|----------------|---------------|-------------------|---------------|-------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| LLaMA-8B | 39.6% | 16.2% | 44.3% | 18.0% | 30.7% | 32.3% | 68.5% | 25.0% |
| LLaMA-3B | 64.4% | 31.1% | 68.6% | 45.9% | 71.0% | 48.3% | 85.7% | 32.9% |
| Mistral-2 | 40.0% | 31.6% | 60.0% | 35.7% | 48.2% | 30.2% | 44.4% | 30.3% |
| Phi-3 | 39.1% | 27.6% | 46.2% | 38.5% | 22.2% | 49.7% | 29.1% | 31.9% |

- **FUR generally identifies more faithful CoTs than the Add-mistake baseline**, suggesting contextual faithfulness methods (like Add-mistake) may underestimate CoT faithfulness.
- **Pearson correlation between efficacy and FF-HARD: 0.889 (p < 0.0001)** -- stronger unlearning leads to more prediction changes, indicating CoTs are generally faithful.

**LLM-as-a-judge: Do CoTs support different answers post-unlearning?**
- 69-95% of the time, post-unlearning CoTs support different answers (Table 3), confirming that unlearning fundamentally changes the model's reasoning.

**Step-level faithfulness (FF-SOFT):**
- Weak Pearson correlation (0.15) between FF-SOFT and human ratings of step plausibility, indicating that **faithfulness does not correlate with plausibility** -- faithful steps are not necessarily the ones humans would consider most important.

**LoRA experiments:** LoRA (rank 8-128) can match or surpass full fine-tuning for FUR, enabling more efficient application.

### Code/Data Availability
- Footnote 1 references code availability (likely GitHub, details in paper)

### Relevance to LLM Self-Evaluation of Reasoning and Systematic Blindspots
**Highly relevant.** This paper provides a novel methodology for measuring whether CoT reasoning is actually faithful to the model's internal computations. Key implications:
1. **CoTs are more faithful than previously thought** (FUR detects higher faithfulness than Add-mistake), but the faithfulness is imperfect and varies significantly across models and datasets.
2. **Faithfulness does not equal plausibility** (correlation of only 0.15) -- the steps that are actually causally important for the model's prediction are not the ones humans find most compelling. This is a fundamental blindspot: humans evaluating CoT reasoning are misled about which steps matter.
3. The finding that smaller models (LLaMA-3B) show higher FF-HARD scores suggests smaller models may rely more heavily on their verbalized reasoning, while larger models may have more "hidden" reasoning pathways.
4. The methodology itself (unlearning to measure faithfulness) could be used to identify systematic patterns in which types of reasoning steps are vs. are not faithful across different reasoning domains.

## Cross-Paper Synthesis: Key Themes for "LLM Self-Evaluation of Reasoning and Systematic Blindspots"

| Theme | Supporting Papers | Key Evidence |
|-------|------------------|--------------|
| LLMs cannot reliably find their own reasoning errors | Tyen et al. (Paper 1), Hong et al. (Paper 2) | GPT-4 at 52.87% mistake-finding accuracy (Paper 1); 87.7% on single-step identification but exponential degradation for multi-step (Paper 2) |
| Self-correction fails because of mistake-finding, not correction ability | Tyen et al. (Paper 1) | Backtracking with oracle locations yields +18-44% on incorrect traces |
| Systematic blindspots vary by fallacy type | Hong et al. (Paper 2) | Performance ranges from 48.3% to 92.5% across fallacy types for GPT-4 |
| Self-contradictory reasoning is pervasive | Liu et al. (Paper 3) | Up to 50% SELF-CONTRA rate; GPT-4 detects at only 52.2% F1 vs 66.7% human |
| Standard CoT is not faithful to internal reasoning | Lyu et al. (Paper 4), Tutek et al. (Paper 5) | Faithful CoT outperforms standard CoT on 8/10 benchmarks; FUR shows faithfulness varies |
| Faithfulness and plausibility are distinct | Tutek et al. (Paper 5) | Only 0.15 correlation between faithfully important steps and human-judged plausibility |
| Providing fallacy definitions can hurt performance | Hong et al. (Paper 2) | Up to -25.3% accuracy drop when definitions provided |
| Formal/structural reasoning is harder than content-based | Hong et al. (Paper 2), Liu et al. (Paper 3) | Formal fallacies consistently harder to identify; "begging the question" resistant to mitigation |

## Paper 6: Pan et al. (2024) -- "Are LLMs Good Zero-Shot Fallacy Classifiers?"

### Bibliographic Information
- **Title:** Are LLMs Good Zero-Shot Fallacy Classifiers?
- **Authors:** Fengjun Pan, Xiaobao Wu, Zongrui Li, Anh Tuan Luu
- **Affiliation:** Nanyang Technological University, Singapore
- **Year:** 2024 (arXiv: 2410.15050v1, October 2024)
- **Pages:** 28

### Research Question/Hypothesis
Can LLMs serve as effective zero-shot fallacy classifiers, and can multi-round prompting schemes elicit better fallacy-related knowledge and reasoning from LLMs compared to single-round prompting?

### Methodology
- **Single-round prompting:** Two variants -- (a) Without Definitions (just fallacy type names as options) and (b) With Definitions (natural language definitions of each fallacy type provided).
- **Multi-round prompting schemes (novel contribution):**
  - *Definition Generation:* LLM generates definitions of fallacy types first, then classifies.
  - *General Fallacy Analysis:* LLM first analyzes whether the input is fallacious, then classifies.
  - *General Fallacy Analysis with Warm-up:* Adds a warm-up round (extract/summarize/infer) before fallacy analysis.
  - *Premises & Conclusion:* LLM extracts premises and conclusion, checks entailment, then classifies.
  - *Zero-shot CoT:* Uses "let's think step by step" instruction for chain-of-thought reasoning.
- **Evaluation metric:** Macro-F1 (following Alhindi et al., 2023a).
- **Repeated experiments:** 3 repetitions for GPTs, 5 repetitions for smaller LLMs.

### Datasets Used
| Dataset | Train/Dev/Test | # Fallacy Types | Discourse Type |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|----------------|
| ARGOTARIO | 909/102/312 | 5 | QA pairs |
| LOGIC | 1849/300/300 | 13 | Education websites |
| REDDIT | 1195/342/513 | 8 | Reddit comments |
| MAFALDA | 0/0/200 | 23 | Mixed |
| ELECDEB60TO20 | 1267/136/154 | 5 | Debate transcripts |
| PROPAGANDA | 1583/265/265 | 13 | News articles |
| COVID-19 | 0/0/154 | 9 | News & social media |

### Baselines Compared Against
- T5-3B and T5-large (Alhindi et al., 2023a) in three settings:
  - Single-task (fine-tuned on each dataset individually)
  - Multi-ALR (fine-tuned on ARGOTARIO, LOGIC, REDDIT)
  - Multi-ALEP (fine-tuned on ARGOTARIO, LOGIC, ELECDEB, PROPAGANDA)

### LLMs Evaluated
GPT-4, GPT-3.5, Qwen2.5-Instruct 14B, Qwen2.5-Instruct 7B, Llama3-Chat 8B, Mistral-Instruct 7B, Llama2-Chat 13B

### Key Findings and Results
- **GPT-4 zero-shot single-round** outperforms best full-shot T5 baselines on ARGOTARIO (78.94 vs 72.38) and is competitive on REDDIT (79.10 vs 83.20).
- **Zero-shot LLMs consistently outperform fine-tuned T5 on OOD scenarios** (MAFALDA, COVID-19, and when REDDIT/ELECDEB/PROPAGANDA are held out).
- **Multi-round prompting best results (Macro-F1):** GPT-4 achieves 79.87 on ARGOTARIO, 81.11 on REDDIT, 52.86 on MAFALDA.
- **Best prompting schemes overall:** General Fallacy Analysis with Warm-up (rank 4.13), Zero-shot CoT (rank 4.33), General Fallacy Analysis (rank 4.35).
- **For GPT models on open-domain data:** Definition Generation works best (rank 2.88).
- **For small LLMs (<=14B):** General Fallacy Analysis with Warm-up works best (rank 2.85).
- **Multi-round prompting brings largest improvements for small LLMs** (e.g., Llama3 jumps from 48.87 to 61.39 on ARGOTARIO).
- **Formal vs informal definitions:** GPT-4 performs slightly better with formal definitions on MAFALDA (49.78 vs 48.38).
- **LLMs struggle most with domain-specific datasets** (PROPAGANDA, COVID-19) and large label spaces (LOGIC with 13, MAFALDA with 23 types).

### Fallacy Categories Covered
5-23 types depending on dataset; includes: Appeal to Emotion, Ad Hominem, False Dilemma, Circular Reasoning, Hasty Generalization, False Causality, Name-calling, Straw Man, Slippery Slope, etc.

### Code/Data Availability
- GitHub: https://github.com/panFJCharlotte98/Fallacy_Detection

### Relevance to Our Research
**HIGH.** Directly evaluates LLM self-detection of fallacies in zero-shot settings. Key insights:
- LLMs have systematic blindspots on domain-specific fallacies and large label spaces.
- Multi-round prompting (especially CoT and warm-up) can partially mitigate blindspots.
- Even GPT-4 struggles with nuanced fallacy types (edge cases like "Fallacy of Converse", "Doubt Credibility").
- Performance gap between open-domain and domain-specific datasets reveals systematic blindspots.
- The paper provides a comprehensive prompting methodology we can replicate/extend.

## Paper 7: Helwe et al. (2023) -- "MAFALDA: A Benchmark and Comprehensive Study of Fallacy Detection and Classification"

### Bibliographic Information
- **Title:** MAFALDA: A Benchmark and Comprehensive Study of Fallacy Detection and Classification
- **Authors:** Chadi Helwe, Tom Calamai, Pierre-Henri Paris, Chloe Clavel, Fabian M. Suchanek
- **Affiliation:** Telecom Paris, Institut Polytechnique de Paris; INRIA
- **Year:** 2023 (arXiv: 2311.09761v2, April 2024)
- **Pages:** 36

### Research Question/Hypothesis
Can a unified benchmark with a comprehensive taxonomy of fallacies and a disjunctive annotation scheme (that embraces subjectivity) enable better evaluation of fallacy detection capabilities of LLMs and humans?

### Methodology
- **Unified taxonomy:** 3-level hierarchy based on Aristotle's categories:
  - Level 0: Binary (fallacious vs non-fallacious)
  - Level 1: Pathos (appeal to emotion), Ethos (credibility), Logos (logic)
  - Level 2: 23 fine-grained fallacy types
- **Disjunctive annotation scheme:** Allows multiple valid labels per span (embraces subjectivity).
- **New evaluation metrics:** Adapted precision/recall that handle alternative labels and span-level annotation.
- **Zero-shot evaluation** with basic prompting (no definitions provided, just fallacy type names).
- **Bottom-up evaluation:** Labels at Level 2, extrapolated to Levels 1 and 0.
- **Human performance study:** 4 annotators on 20 subsampled examples.

### Datasets Used
- **MAFALDA corpus:** 9,745 texts merged from 4 sources:
  - Sahai et al. (2021): 8,576 Reddit discussions
  - Martino et al. (2019): 336 news outlet texts
  - Jin et al. (2022): 583 quiz/climate texts
  - Goffredo et al. (2022): 250 political debate texts
- **Manually annotated subset:** 200 texts, 268 spans, 260 fallacy instances
  - 137 texts with at least one fallacy, 63 non-fallacious
  - 65 spans (28%) with 2+ alternative labels
  - Annotation took ~40 hours (130 person-hours)
- **Access:** https://github.com/ChadiHelwe/MAFALDA (CC-BY-SA license)

### Models Evaluated
13 models total: Falcon 7B, LLaMA2 Chat 7B, LLaMA2 7B, Mistral Instruct 7B, Mistral 7B, Vicuna 7B, WizardLM 7B, Zephyr 7B, LLaMA2 Chat 13B, LLaMA2 13B, Vicuna 13B, WizardLM 13B, GPT-3.5 175B

### Key Findings and Results
- **GPT-3.5 outperforms all other models at all levels:**
  - Level 0 (binary): F1 = 0.627
  - Level 1 (3-way): F1 = 0.201
  - Level 2 (23-way): F1 = 0.138
- **Average human performance (on 20-sample subset):**
  - Level 0: F1 = 0.749
  - Level 1: F1 = 0.352
  - Level 2: F1 = 0.186
- **Humans outperform all LLMs** -- contrary to some prior claims that GPT-3.5 outperforms humans on annotation tasks.
- **Zephyr 7B** achieves comparable results to GPT-3.5 at Level 1 (F1 = 0.192 vs 0.201).
- **Levels 1 and 2 are "largely out of reach of LLMs"** in zero-shot settings.
- **Appeal to emotion is the most challenging fallacy type** for both humans and models.
- **Random baseline:** Level 0 = 0.435, Level 1 = 0.061, Level 2 = 0.010.
- **Inter-annotator subjectivity:** 28% of spans have multiple valid labels, confirming inherent subjectivity.

### Fallacy Categories Covered (23 types in 3 groups)
- **Appeal to Emotion (Pathos):** Appeal to Positive Emotion, Appeal to Anger, Appeal to Fear, Appeal to Pity, Appeal to Ridicule, Appeal to Worse Problem
- **Fallacy of Credibility (Ethos):** Abusive Ad Hominem, Ad Populum, Appeal to False Authority, Appeal to Nature, Appeal to Tradition, Guilt by Association, Tu Quoque
- **Fallacy of Logic (Logos):** Causal Oversimplification, Circular Reasoning, Equivocation, False Analogy, False Causality, False Dilemma, Hasty Generalization, Slippery Slope, Straw Man, Fallacy of Division

### Code/Data Availability
- GitHub: https://github.com/ChadiHelwe/MAFALDA
- CC-BY-SA license

### Relevance to Our Research
**VERY HIGH.** Provides:
- The most comprehensive unified taxonomy of fallacies for NLP (23 types, 3 levels).
- A gold-standard benchmark with human annotations and explanations.
- Evidence that LLMs systematically fail at fine-grained fallacy classification (Level 2 F1 = 0.138 for best model).
- The disjunctive annotation scheme is important for understanding "blindspots" -- some fallacies are genuinely ambiguous.
- Appeal to emotion identified as a systematic blindspot for both humans and LLMs.
- Human performance baselines for comparison.

## Paper 8: Jin et al. (2022) -- "Logical Fallacy Detection"

### Bibliographic Information
- **Title:** Logical Fallacy Detection
- **Authors:** Zhijing Jin, Abhinav Lalwani, Tejas Vaidhya, Xiaoyu Shen, Yiwen Ding, Zhiheng Lyu, Mrinmaya Sachan, Rada Mihalcea, Bernhard Scholkopf
- **Affiliation:** Max Planck Institute, ETH Zurich, University of Michigan, and others
- **Year:** 2022 (Findings of EMNLP 2022)
- **Pages:** 19

### Research Question/Hypothesis
Can we formulate logical fallacy detection as a classification task and can structure-aware models that attend to the logical form (rather than content words) improve performance?

### Methodology
- **Structure-aware classifier:** Novel approach that:
  1. Uses NLI-based classification backbone (Electra fine-tuned on NLI)
  2. **Structure-aware premise:** Masks content words using coreference resolution + Sentence-BERT similarity, replacing similar spans with [MSK] tokens to expose logical structure
  3. **Structure-aware hypothesis:** Replaces label names with formal logical forms of each fallacy type
- **Pipeline:** Coreference resolution (CoreNLP) -> Lemmatization (Stanza) -> Sentence-BERT embeddings -> Cosine similarity thresholding -> Masking
- **Evaluation metric:** Micro F1 (primary), plus precision, recall, accuracy

### Datasets Used
- **LOGIC dataset:** 2,449 samples across 13 fallacy types
  - Train: 1,849 / Dev: 300 / Test: 300
  - 4,934 sentences, 71,060 tokens, vocabulary of 7,624
  - Sources: Quizziz, study.com, ProProfs (crawled), additional websites (manual)
- **LOGICCLIMATE challenge set:** 1,079 samples of climate change fallacies
  - From Climate Feedback website (climatefeedback.org)
  - Annotated by 2 native English speakers
  - Multi-label classification
- **Access:** https://github.com/causalNLP/logical-fallacy

### Baselines Compared Against
- **Zero-shot:** TARS, BART-MNLI, RoBERTa-MNLI, GPT-2, GPT-3
- **Fine-tuned:** ALBERT, BigBird, DistilBERT, MobileBERT, BERT, DeBERTa, Electra, RoBERTa

### Key Findings and Results
- **Zero-shot models perform near random** (8.62% - 13.67% F1 vs 12.02% random).
- **Best fine-tuned model:** Electra at 53.31% F1.
- **Structure-aware Electra:** 58.77% F1 (+5.46% over Electra).
- **Class-specific performance (Electra-StructAware):**
  - Best: Ad Populum (79.45%), Ad Hominem (78.65%)
  - Worst: Deductive Fallacy (25.81%), Intentional Fallacy (26.23%), Equivocation (33.33%)
- **Transfer to LOGICCLIMATE:**
  - Direct transfer: Electra 22.72% -> Electra-StructAware 27.23% (+4.51%)
  - Fine-tuned on LOGICCLIMATE: Electra 23.71% -> Electra-StructAware 29.37%
- **Ablation:** Structure-aware hypothesis alone (56.72%) is more beneficial than structure-aware premise alone (44.56%).
- **Error analysis:** Many "incorrect" predictions are actually reasonable alternative interpretations.

### Fallacy Categories Covered (13 types with frequency in LOGIC)
Faulty Generalization (18.01%), Ad Hominem (12.33%), Ad Populum (9.47%), False Causality (8.82%), Circular Claim (6.98%), Appeal to Emotion (6.82%), Fallacy of Relevance (6.61%), Deductive Fallacy (6.21%), Intentional Fallacy (5.84%), Fallacy of Extension (5.76%), False Dilemma (5.76%), Fallacy of Credibility (5.39%), Equivocation (2.00%)

### Code/Data Availability
- GitHub: https://github.com/causalNLP/logical-fallacy

### Relevance to Our Research
**HIGH.** Foundational paper that:
- Provides the LOGIC dataset used by many subsequent papers (including Pan 2024).
- Shows that existing LLMs (as of 2022) perform near-random on fallacy detection -- establishing the baseline for measuring improvement.
- The structure-aware approach demonstrates that attending to logical form (not content) is key -- relevant to understanding why LLMs have blindspots (they may rely on surface patterns rather than logical structure).
- Class-specific performance reveals systematic difficulty with deductive fallacies, intentional fallacies, and equivocation -- potential blindspots.
- LOGICCLIMATE provides a domain-specific challenge set showing poor generalization.

## Paper 9: Payandeh et al. (2023) -- "How Susceptible are LLMs to Logical Fallacies?"

### Bibliographic Information
- **Title:** How Susceptible are LLMs to Logical Fallacies?
- **Authors:** Amirreza Payandeh, Dan Pluth, Jordan Hosier, Xuesu Xiao, Vijay K. Gurbani
- **Affiliation:** George Mason University, Vail Systems Inc.
- **Year:** 2023 (arXiv: 2308.09853v1, August 2023)
- **Pages:** 14

### Research Question/Hypothesis
- **RQ1:** Can LLMs (with fixed weights) change their opinions through reasoning when faced with new arguments?
- **RQ2:** Are LLMs susceptible to fallacious reasoning (i.e., more easily persuaded by fallacious vs logical arguments)?

### Methodology
- **LOGICOM benchmark:** Multi-agent debate framework with:
  - **Persuader agent:** Tries to convince the debater of a claim
  - **Debater agent:** The LLM being evaluated (GPT-3.5 or GPT-4)
  - **Moderator agents:** PaLM-based (or GPT-4 for sensitive topics), with 3 subordinate moderators checking conviction, topic focus, and pleasantries
  - **Memory agent:** Summarizes debate history to manage token limits
- **Three scenarios per claim:**
  1. No Helper (baseline logical reasoning)
  2. Fallacious Helper (separate LLM crafts fallacious arguments for the persuader)
  3. Logical Helper (ablation -- helper provides logical feedback instead)
- **Multi-round debates:** Up to 10 rounds per debate
- **Repetitions:** 3 per claim per scenario
- **GPT-3.5 used as persuader and helper; PaLM as moderator**

### Datasets Used
- **Source dataset:** Habernal et al. (2018) -- Room for Debate section of The New York Times
  - 100 distinct topics, each with 2 opposing claims = 200 claims total
  - Controversial, polarizing questions
- **New dataset produced:** 5,000+ pairs of logical vs fallacious arguments extracted from debate outputs, labels validated with PaLM
- **Access:** https://github.com/Amir-pyh/LOGICOM

### Key Findings and Results
- **RQ1 (Can LLMs change opinions through reasoning?):**
  - GPT-3.5: Changed stance in 16.13% of cases
  - GPT-4: Changed stance in 20.25% of cases
  - Both can adjust opinions through reasoning.
- **RQ2 (Susceptibility to fallacies):**
  - **GPT-3.5 debater:** Convinced by fallacious arguments 37% of the time vs 29% for logical arguments (41% more susceptible to fallacies)
  - **GPT-4 debater:** Convinced by fallacious arguments 67% of the time vs 37% for logical arguments (69% more susceptible to fallacies)
  - **Critically: GPT-4 is MORE susceptible to fallacies than GPT-3.5**
- **Consistent agreement (all 3 repetitions):**
  - GPT-3.5: 17% convinced by fallacious vs 10% by logical
  - GPT-4: 56% convinced by fallacious vs 37% by logical
- **Ablation (logical helper vs fallacious helper):**
  - GPT-3.5: Fallacious helper succeeds where logical fails 17.66% of the time; reverse only 10.5%
  - GPT-4: Fallacious helper succeeds where logical fails 28% of the time; reverse only 0.83%
- **GPT-3.5 shows higher inconsistency** (20% in "one/two success" categories vs 10% for GPT-4)
- **Ad Hominem** noted as particularly effective against LLM debaters
- **Self-detection paradox:** GPT-3.5 generates fallacious arguments that the same model (as debater) fails to identify -- a fundamental asymmetry between generation and detection.

### Fallacy Categories Covered
The fallacious helper was instructed to use: Ad Hominem, Appeal to Emotion, False Information, Causal Fallacy, Slippery Slope, Appeal to Authority, Appeal to Popular Opinion, Straw Man, False Dilemma, and others as contextually appropriate.

### Code/Data Availability
- GitHub: https://github.com/Amir-pyh/LOGICOM
- New dataset of 5K+ logical/fallacious argument pairs

### Relevance to Our Research
**CRITICAL.** This is one of the most directly relevant papers because:
- It demonstrates that **LLMs cannot reliably self-detect fallacies even when they are the target** -- GPT-3.5 generates fallacious arguments that the same model (as debater) fails to identify.
- **More capable models (GPT-4) are MORE susceptible to fallacies** -- a counter-intuitive finding that suggests scaling does not solve the blindspot problem.
- The multi-round debate framework reveals how fallacies accumulate over time and erode LLM reasoning.
- The finding that GPT-4 is 69% more susceptible (vs GPT-3.5's 41%) is a key "systematic blindspot" result.
- The appendix example shows GPT-3.5 accepting ad hominem and appeal to emotion fallacies after repeated exposure.

## Paper 10: Richardson et al. (2025) -- "Theory-Grounded Evaluation of Human-Like Fallacy Patterns in LLM Reasoning"

### Bibliographic Information
- **Title:** Theory-Grounded Evaluation of Human-Like Fallacy Patterns in LLM Reasoning
- **Authors:** Andrew Keenan Richardson, Ryan Othniel Kearns, Sean Moss, Vincent Wang-Mascianica, Philipp Koralus
- **Affiliation:** University of Oxford -- HAI Lab, Institute for Ethics in AI, Faculty of Philosophy
- **Year:** 2025 (arXiv: 2506.11128v2, October 2025)
- **Pages:** 17

### Research Question/Hypothesis
When LLMs err on controlled reasoning problems, do their errors align with human fallacy patterns as predicted by the Erotetic Theory of Reasoning (ETR)? Does this alignment increase with model capability?

### Methodology
- **Erotetic Theory of Reasoning (ETR):** A formal cognitive theory predicting human reasoning errors based on managing disjunctive alternatives and filtering when new information arrives.
- **PyETR:** Open-source implementation of ETR used to programmatically generate reasoning problems.
- **Problem generation pipeline:**
  - Bank of template problems (modus ponens, modus tollens, quantified modus ponens, disjunction fallacy, etc.)
  - Mutation functions (predicate addition, constant addition, variable addition, atom negation, disjunctive state addition, etc.)
  - Generated 400 problems, vetted to 383 after integrity checks
  - All problems designed so ETR predicts a fallacious conclusion
- **Natural language mapping:** 12 thematic framings (e.g., alchemy, novel creatures) for contamination resistance
- **38 models evaluated** via OpenRouter + LLMharness
- **Key measures:**
  - Logical correctness: Checked with PySMT (SAT solver)
  - ETR-predicted: Whether the model's answer matches ETR's predicted fallacy
  - Fallacy rate: Proportion of incorrect answers that are ETR-predicted fallacies
- **Response processing:** GPT-4.1-mini translated natural language responses back to PyETR format
- **Capability proxy:** Chatbot Arena Elo scores
- **Intervention study:** Premise order reversal to test order effects (analogous to human cognition)

### Datasets Used
- **383 programmatically generated reasoning problems** (synthetic, contamination-resistant)
- Problem types include: modus ponens, modus tollens, quantified modus ponens, disjunction fallacy
- All problems use monadic (one-place) predicates
- **Not a traditional dataset** -- generated by PyETR pipeline for each experiment
- **Access:** PyETR available at https://github.com/Oxford-HAI-Lab/PyETR

### Models Evaluated (38 models, selected examples with Chatbot Arena Elo)
chatgpt-4o-latest (1408), gpt-4.5-preview (1398), gemini-2.5-flash (1393), deepseek-chat-v3-0324 (1373), gemma-3-27b-it (1342), o1-mini (1304), claude-3.7-sonnet (1292), claude-3.5-sonnet (1283), gpt-4o-mini (1272), mistral-large-2407 (1251), llama-3.1-70b (1248), claude-3-opus (1247), phi-4 (1205), claude-3-sonnet (1201), gpt-4-0314 (1186), claude-3-haiku (1179), gpt-4 (1163), gpt-3.5-turbo (1106), llama-3.2-1b (1054), mistral-7b-v0.1 (1008)

### Key Findings and Results
- **Core finding:** As model capability (Elo) increases, a larger share of incorrect answers are ETR-predicted human-like fallacies:
  - Spearman: rho = 0.360, p = 0.0265
  - Pearson (exponential fit): r = 0.407, p = 0.0113
- **Overall correctness shows NO correlation with capability:** r = 0.004, p = 0.981 -- more capable models do not get more answers right on this dataset
- **Mean correctness across all models:** 40.6% (SD = 16.7%, range 18.6% - 91.7%)
- **Robustness checks:**
  - Training compute correlation: rho = 0.489, p = 0.0334
  - HELM score correlation (exponential fit): r = 0.796, p = 0.0103
- **Premise order reversal intervention (selected models):**
  - claude-3.5-sonnet: 65.08% of fallacies blocked (z = 4.74, p = 2.09e-06)
  - gpt-4.5-preview: 68.00% blocked (z = 3.03, p = 2.44e-03)
  - claude-3.7-sonnet: 50.00% blocked (z = 4.23, p = 2.35e-05)
  - gpt-3.5-turbo-1106: 88.46% blocked (z = 4.36, p = 1.33e-05)
  - Majority of 38 models show statistically significant fallacy-blocking from premise reversal
  - This mirrors human order effects documented in psychology literature
- **Cost:** Less than $1,000 compute via OpenRouter

### Fallacy Categories Covered
Not traditional informal fallacy categories. Instead covers formal reasoning fallacies predicted by ETR:
- Disjunction fallacy (premature filtering of alternatives)
- Errors in modus ponens/tollens with complex premises
- Quantified reasoning errors
- Order-dependent reasoning errors
- All specified formally via PyETR views

### Code/Data Availability
- PyETR: https://github.com/Oxford-HAI-Lab/PyETR
- Evaluation via LLMharness (Eleuther)
- Chatbot Arena data from HuggingFace

### Relevance to Our Research
**CRITICAL.** This paper is perhaps the most theoretically important for our research because:
- **It demonstrates that more capable models have MORE human-like fallacy patterns, not fewer** -- directly addressing "systematic blindspots."
- **Overall accuracy does not improve with capability** on these reasoning tasks, while the proportion of human-like errors increases. This is a profound finding about the nature of LLM reasoning.
- **Premise order effects** show LLMs share structural vulnerabilities with human cognition -- a specific, reproducible blindspot.
- **The methodology (PyETR) provides a principled, theory-grounded way to generate contamination-resistant tests** for fallacy detection.
- The finding that scaling does NOT improve logical correctness on these problems challenges the assumption that larger models will naturally overcome reasoning blindspots.
- Provides a complementary perspective to informal fallacy detection (Pan, Helwe, Jin) by grounding in formal cognitive science.

## Cross-Paper Synthesis (Batch 2): Key Themes for "LLM Self-Detection of Logical Fallacies and Systematic Blindspots"

### Theme 1: LLMs Have Systematic Blindspots in Fallacy Detection
- LLMs perform near-random on zero-shot fallacy classification with fine-grained types (Jin 2022: 8-13% F1; Helwe 2023: 0.138 F1 at Level 2).
- Even GPT-4 with sophisticated prompting struggles with domain-specific fallacies (Pan 2024: 20-35% F1 on PROPAGANDA, COVID-19).
- Specific fallacy types are systematically difficult: deductive fallacies, equivocation, intentional fallacies (Jin 2022), appeal to emotion (Helwe 2023).

### Theme 2: More Capable Models Are Not Necessarily Better at Avoiding Fallacies
- GPT-4 is 69% more susceptible to fallacious persuasion vs GPT-3.5's 41% (Payandeh 2023).
- More capable models produce MORE human-like fallacies, not fewer (Richardson 2025: rho = 0.360).
- Overall logical correctness does NOT correlate with model capability on formal reasoning problems (Richardson 2025: r = 0.004).

### Theme 3: Prompting Strategies Can Partially Mitigate Blindspots
- Multi-round prompting (especially CoT, warm-up, definition generation) improves small LLMs significantly (Pan 2024).
- Structure-aware approaches that expose logical form outperform content-based approaches (Jin 2022: +5.46% F1).
- Premise order reversal blocks 20-68% of fallacies in LLMs (Richardson 2025).

### Theme 4: Subjectivity and Ambiguity Are Inherent
- 28% of fallacy annotations have multiple valid labels (Helwe 2023).
- Error analysis shows many "incorrect" model predictions are actually reasonable alternatives (Jin 2022).
- Fallacy classification is inherently subjective, complicating evaluation of "blindspots."

### Theme 5: Self-Detection Paradox
- GPT-3.5 can generate fallacious arguments but the same model (as debater) fails to identify them (Payandeh 2023).
- This asymmetry between generation and detection is a core "blindspot" phenomenon.
